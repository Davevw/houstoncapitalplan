5.Strategic Direction — Buyout + Total Sale Optionality

The project is being made more viable through two parallel exit pathways, each independently supportive of the partner’s repatriation of invested capital with a reasonable return.

Path A — Partner Buyout
  • Structure: Clean cash transaction acquiring 100% of partner’s interest in Bissonnet 136, LLC; full mutual release of all obligations between the parties
  • Pricing approach: Buyout sized close to land contribution and deemed capital amount — same economics partner would realize if remaining in the deal — not assumed at $14M
  • Debt sources: Walker Dunlop and NEXA Advisory Services for senior debt covering buyout and forward infrastructure in single capitalization
  • Sponsor in-kind contribution: MSW permit revocation contributed to the new venture as a structuring lever
  • Target close: Q4 2026, aligning with October construction start
  • Rationale: Move forward and exit partner desiring to repatriate invested dollars and receive reasonable return
Path B — Total Project Sale (Parallel Track)
  • Structure: Marketing the project through private channels for total sale to a single bulk acquirer
  • Audience: Institutional and strategic acquirers seeking a fully-entitled, MUD-approved, infrastructure-ready master-planned project
  • Asset story: 136 acres, 30 lots, $39.4M gross inventory, MUD 584 approved, $23.4M reimbursement available, active sales pipeline already converting to contracts
  • Coordination with Path A: Both paths advance in parallel; total sale would substitute for both buyout and forward development; either path repatriates partner capital
Why both paths in parallel: Continuing development and lot sales increases asset value, attracts more credible bulk-sale interest, and strengthens the buyout pricing case. The two-pronged approach is not contradictory — each path reinforces the other and preserves maximum optionality for all stakeholders.

Buyout consideration is sized to partner’s land contribution and deemed capital position, plus the project’s incurred costs as carried in the v13 model. The structure delivers the partner the economic outcome of remaining in the deal, in cash, at exit — without speculative valuation premiums or commitments to amounts that haven’t been agreed.

6.2 Capital History — The Asset Is Financeable

Over the prior 18 months, CBRE (John Fenoglio) circulated the project to three institutional lenders — OZK Bank, LSM (Lionheart), and Allegiant — all three of whom expressed firm interest. Each was blocked at closing by the JV’s unwillingness to proceed with debt as a reasonable source for construction. Walker Dunlop and Zarche Group produced the same pattern. The previous overseas equity group (ARESC Holdings) did not materialize. The pattern across five capital sources and three years is consistent: the underlying asset clears underwriting; the JV’s capital-structure preferences have been the gating constraint.

The buyout (or total sale) resolves the recurring constraint. New capital is not being asked to take a risk no one else would take — it is being asked to close the deal that OZK, LSM, and Allegiant all wanted but could not reach. Either exit path removes the constraint that has historically prevented debt from being deployed, and unlocks capital that has already been identified.

Important: The continuing existence of the MSW permit does not affect development progress. It is an SKA workstream proceeding in parallel and poses no risk to the construction schedule, MUD bond issuance, sales execution, or buyout / total-sale exit pathways.
